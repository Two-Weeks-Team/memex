It doesn't wait to be searched

The panel above isn't a search box you typed into. It's a situation — and the memory rose to meet it. That's the difference between an archive and institutional memory: an archive waits; memory arrives.

Context, not queries

Recall is triggered by what you're doing now — the file, the error, the topic — not by a query you have to think to write.

Similarity, not keywords

Backed by Qdrant vector space, past sessions return because they're close in meaning to your present moment — even when no words match.

Lifelike timing

Memories arrive on their own irregular cadence — the quiet "oh, right" of recognition — not all at once on a click.

Governance, framed honestly

Autonomous recall is the point. Control is what makes it safe to adopt. Here's exactly what Memex does today — and what it does not claim to do.

  • ✓Local-first by defaultYou point Memex at your own session files (~/.claude/projects/**/*.jsonl, legacy transcripts, history.jsonl). The corpus you build is yours.
  • ✓No LLM at runtimeRetrieval is vector search over Qdrant — not a chatbot and not a model call. Your history isn't sent to a model to answer.
  • ✓You own the corpusClaude Code silently migrates and deletes old sessions; Memex preserves them under your control so institutional knowledge isn't quietly lost.
  • ✓Self-hostableA server / Docker variant runs Qdrant, the web UI, and the MCP server in one image — deploy it where your data is allowed to live.

What we don't claim. Memex does not ship SSO, SAML, SCIM, or a SOC 2 report today, and we won't pretend otherwise. The honest enterprise story right now is data residency by construction: it runs where you run it, and nothing leaves your machine or your self-hosted instance unless you wire it to. If your buying process needs formal attestations, that's a roadmap conversation — not a checkbox we'll fake.
